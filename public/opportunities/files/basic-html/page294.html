All of them are learnable by anyone willing to pay attention.I write this from the position of someone who has watched the Nigerian and broaderAfrican digital asset community lose money to scams that were preventable, not becausethe victims were foolish, but because nobody had explained clearly and directly what thethreats looked like and how to avoid them. That is what this chapter attempts to do.The Threat Landscape: An Honest MapBefore examining individual threat categories, it is worth mapping the threat landscapeaccurately, not to create anxiety, but to calibrate the relative magnitude of different risks,because most people are afraid of the wrong things.The threats that receive the most media attention, exchange hacks, protocol exploits, andsophisticated technical attacks, are real and significant, but they are not where mostindividual investors lose money. Exchange hacks affect you only if you keep assets onexchanges, and the risk can be largely eliminated by the simple discipline of not keepingassets on exchanges beyond what you need for active trading. Protocol exploits affect youonly if you are using the affected protocol, and the risk can be managed throughdiversification and protocol selection.The threats that actually account for the majority of individual losses are social: investmentscams, exit scams, phishing attacks, and romance scams. These succeed not throughtechnical sophistication but through psychological manipulation, the exploitation of greed,fear, loneliness, and the very human desire to believe that an extraordinary opportunity isreal. Understanding this changes where you should focus your defensive attention.The threat landscape also varies by participant profile. A long-term Bitcoin holder whouses a hardware wallet and does not interact with DeFi protocols faces a fundamentallydifferent threat profile than an active DeFi user who bridges assets across multiple chains,connects their wallet to new protocols regularly, and keeps significant balances in hotwallets for operational convenience. Calibrating your security practices to your actualbehaviour, rather than to some idealised set of best practices that you will not actuallyfollow, is the most pragmatic approach.%u2014 294 %u2014
