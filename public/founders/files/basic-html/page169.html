The cross-border payment marketis expected to triple from 329 billion dollars in 2025 to 1 trillion dollars by 2035. These arenot speculative projections. They are the compounded effect of demographic trends, ayoung, digitally-native, increasingly connected population, which is already visible incurrent transaction data.The founders who will capture the largest share of this opportunity are building in threespecific areas that AfCFTA%u2019s trajectory favours:1. Payment infrastructure for intra-African commerce %u2014 The gap between where value flows %u2014 instablecoins, P2P rails, and mobile money, and where institutional settlement infrastructure exists isan engineering problem that private sector founders can solve. Building the bridges between PAPSS,mobile money systems, stablecoin rails, and the VASP-to-bank interfaces that allow cross-bordercommerce to settle reliably and cheaply is the most durable infrastructure investment in Africandigital finance.2. Multi-jurisdiction compliance infrastructure %u2014 The complexity of operating in multiple Africanregulatory environments simultaneously is a genuine barrier, but it is a soluble one, and the founderswho solve it for themselves can sell the solution to others. A compliance platform that managesNigerian NFIU obligations, Ghanaian FIC requirements, Kenyan KRA reporting, and South African FSCAmonitoring from a single interface is a product that every African VASP with cross-border ambitionswill eventually need.3. Identity and trust infrastructure %u2014 Cross-border commerce requires cross-border trust, the abilityfor a Lagos merchant to verify that an Accra customer is who they say they are, and vice versa. TheAfCFTA Digital Trade Protocol%u2019s cross-border e-KYC provisions are a mandate to create thisinfrastructure. The private sector founders who build it, before the government mandate createsdemand pressure, will have a first-mover advantage in the continental identity market that noincumbent financial institution currently occupies.The pan-African regulatory convergence is coming. It will not arrive on the timeline thatAfCFTA%u2019s optimists have projected. The Digital Trade Protocol will take longer to ratify,longer to implement, and longer to translate into operating reality than anyannouncement suggests.
