                                    THE FOUNDER'S GUIDE TO BUILDING IN REGULATED MARKETSThe Digital Trade Protocol is an important step in the direction of regulatoryharmonisation, but its ratification and implementation will take years, and its actual effecton the operating environment for digital financial services businesses will depend on howeach member state translates its obligations into domestic law. The Protocol%u2019s mostconsequential provisions, on mobile money interoperability, cross-border e-KYCauthentication, and PAPSS integration mandates, are mandates on governments, notpermissions for businesses. They create the framework within which businesses caneventually operate more fluidly. They do not create that fluidity immediately.The founders who are waiting for AfCFTA to resolve their multi-jurisdictioncompliance complexity will wait indefinitely. The founders who are buildingmulti-jurisdiction compliance infrastructure now, in advance of the framework,will be the ones who can take advantage of it fastest when it arrives.The Infrastructure Gap: The Real BarrierThe second important correction to the optimistic AfCFTA narrative is about what theactual barrier to pan-African digital commerce is. It is not primarily regulation.Cross-border payments through traditional channels currently cost between seven andtwenty percent of the transaction value and take three to five days to clear. These costs arenot primarily the product of regulatory complexity. They are the product of infrastructurefragmentation: the absence of direct settlement mechanisms between African currencies,the reliance on correspondent banking routes through New York or London, and theinadequate interoperability between the continent%u2019s payment systems.A Nigerian company trying to pay a Ghanaian supplier in cedis, or to receive paymentfrom a Kenyan customer in shillings, faces a series of infrastructure bottlenecks that noregulatory harmonisation effort directly addresses. The naira must be converted to dollars,routed through an international correspondent bank, converted to cedis, and settledthrough the Ghanaian banking system, incurring conversion fees, counterparty risk, andsettlement delay at every step.
